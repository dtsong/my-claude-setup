## Em-dash restriction (HARD CONSTRAINT)

Em-dashes (—) are a recognizable AI-generation signal. Across the entire resume:

- **Hard cap: 0–1 em-dashes total.** Aim for zero.
- **Default replacements:** comma, semicolon, or sentence restructuring
- **Never use " — " to join a cause-and-effect clause.** Use comma or "which" instead.

**Examples:**
- ✗ "...optimize data flows across Cegid, SAP, Tibco, and Scale — reducing manual investigation time..."
- ✓ "...optimize data flows across Cegid, SAP, Tibco, and Scale, reducing manual investigation time..."
- ✗ "Built ITSM infrastructure — reduced ticket volume by 40%..."
- ✓ "Built ITSM infrastructure; reduced ticket volume by 40%..."

The en-dash (–) used in compound terms like "Cegid–Salesforce" and date ranges is fine and should be preserved. This rule targets em-dashes only.

## Preferred verb palette

**Use freely** (Alan's voice):
- Managed, led, delivered, built, implemented, adopted
- Designed, engineered, automated, deployed, oversaw
- Created, maintained, partnered, coordinated, integrated
- Reduced, improved, cut, achieved, accelerated, enabled

**Use cautiously** (propose to Alan first):
- Owned, drove, spearheaded — sometimes fits, but ask before substituting
- "Architected" — only when JD uses the word

**Avoid entirely:**
- "Results-oriented" / "self-starter" / "team player" / "passionate about"
- "Synergies" / "value-add" / "best-in-class"
- "Familiar with" — be specific or omit

## Cover letter voice

- First person, active voice, Alan's measured tone
- Specific to the role (company name, one concrete thing about them)
- Lead with why-them, not why-me
- One concrete proof point per paragraph from the verified-safe list
- Close with location: "based in San Diego, available for in-person interviews"
- No "I am writing to apply for" filler
- No "Please find my resume attached" sign-off
- 180–220 words
- **Same em-dash restriction applies.** 0–1 em-dashes maximum.
